--- layout: slides --- class: center, middle # CME 216, ME 343 - Spring 2020 ## Eric Darve, ICME ![:width 40%](../Stanford.jpg) --- class: center, middle ## Machine learning in computational engineering Computational engineering has unique requirements, which are different from image and signal processing, or computer vision. --- class: center, middle **Regression** is a much more common application than classification. In most cases, we are interested in computing a pressure, velocity, or strain field. --- class: center, middle The required **accuracy** is typically high. For example, we often expect errors in the range $10^{-4}$–$10^{-3}$ or less to have confidence in the prediction. --- class: center, middle Data is more limited --- class: middle Data in computational engineering is typically obtained either through - experiments, which may be expensive and typically do not generate large amounts of data, or - high-fidelity computer simulations. The advent of automated sensors and large-scale parallel computers is changing this landscape. --- class: center, middle We are often interested in quantifying the **uncertainty** in the prediction and deriving intervals of confidence for our prediction. --- class: center, middle We can **leverage equations from physics,** conservation laws, physical principles, which can inform our learning algorithms. This is different from other applications in computer science for example where most of the learning is restricted to learning from data. --- class: middle See some [recent research papers](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?hl=en&as_sdt=0%2C5&q=physics+informed+deep+learning&btnG=) on this topic.
